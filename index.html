Biography

Diogo Maicon Krevoniz Balduino holds a master's degree in Applied Linguistics (2022) from the Interdisciplinary Graduate Program in Applied Linguistics (PIPGLA) at the Federal University of Rio de Janeiro (UFRJ/Capes). He also holds a BA in Languages and Literature with a specialization in English (2019) from the University of Vale do Rio dos Sinos (UNISINOS/ProUni). In 2022, he was awarded a Fulbright grant. He served as an Teaching Assistant of Portuguese Language at Washington and Lee University in the United States. He taught English to different age groups, including Elementary, Middle, and High School. Additionally, Diogo has taught at language programs, such as the Access E2C Program, which is sponsored by the US Embassy in Brazil. Understanding education as a political act, his teaching practice is committed to promoting critical and citizen linguistic education. In the classroom, he aims to create a welcoming space for dialogue and affection, engaged with diversity and social transformations. His current research interests include public health promotion policies and discourses on methods of sexually transmitted infection prevention, construction of identities, gender, and sexuality from interdisciplinary perspectives that establish dialogue between Applied Linguistics, Linguistic Anthropology, Medical Anthropology, Interactional Sociolinguistics, Conversation Analysis, and Queer Linguistics.

Research

2020 – 2022: Text circulation, interaction, and LGBTQ identities in contemporary Brazil

Description: This research project seeks to explore the mechanisms through which cis-heteronormativity functions as a means of shaping interpretations and how these norms are negotiated during social interactions. The investigation will be structured into two phases. Firstly, an online ethnographic study (Rahm-Skågeby, 2011) will be conducted to examine the dissemination of discriminatory and neoconservative content within public profiles on social media platforms during the period from 2011 to 2018. This timeframe coincides with a notable escalation in discourses opposing sexual minorities, as indicated by various recent analyses (Borba, 2020). The analysis will incorporate the theoretical frameworks of "Textual Trajectories" (Blommaert, 2005) and "Entextualization" (Bauman; Briggs, 1990). Subsequently, the research will document spontaneous interactions among young adults identifying as members of the LGBTQ+ community, captured through audio and video recordings. These recordings will be transcribed, and the analysis will integrate concepts such as "Intersubjectivity Tactics" (Bucholtz; Hall, 2003; 2004; 2005), "Indexicality" (Ochs, 1992), "Contextualization Cues" (Gumperz, 1982), "Conversation Analysis" (Sacks; Schegloff; Jefferson, 1974), and "Membership Categorization Analysis" (Sacks, 1992). Additionally, the examination of identities as performative (Butler, 2003) will inform the analysis. Given the prevalence of homophobic rhetoric in the public domain, the study aims to investigate how LGBTQ+ individuals navigate these discourses within the context of their daily lives. It aims to explore the strategies employed by individuals to either challenge or reaffirm these discourses in their everyday interactions. Ultimately, the research seeks to elucidate how LGBTQ+ individuals engage with oppressive discourses and the impact of these discourses on their identity formation.

Status: Completed; Nature: Academic Research; Supervisor: Dr. Rodrigo Borba.

2018 - 2018: Gender and Sexual identity negotiation in pre-party conversation

Description: Based on the theoretical and methodological perspective of Conversation Analysis (Sacks; Schegloff; Jefferson, 1974) and Membership Categorization Analysis (Sacks, 1992), this research study project departs from the understanding that social identity are locally constructed and negotiated and it is done by activating categories (e.g., ethnicity, gender and sexuality) in interaction. To understand the phenomena socially demonstrated in interaction, the analysis within both CA and MCA takes an emic perspective (Pike, 1967; Malabarba, 2015), i.e., the analysis is done by looking to the ways in which the participants interpret what is said and done by each other. The database will be composed by interactions taking place during a weekend trip and pre-party conversation among a group of friends who identify themselves as belonging to the LGBT+ community (Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender and non-heteronormative individuals). The goal of the study will be to understand what identity are performed in the interactions and how gender and sexual identities become interactionally relevant and how they are co-constructed, negotiated, enacted, ratified and challenged. The study will contribute to Brazilian CA studies on social identity and to bring representativeness to Queer individuals.

Status: Completed; Nature: Academic Research; Supervisor: Dr. Taiane Malabarba.
